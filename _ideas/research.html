Do judgments of beauty influence moral behavior? Although it seems obvious that beauty and morality should be independent, our research shows that people with facial anomalies, such as scars or paralysis, are seen as having worse social traits, including morally-relevant characteristics like untrustworthiness.

We also examine how individuals are judged based on other physical features such as body weight. We're interested in whether we can modify these sorts of biases by presenting images in new contexts — for instance, when we apply brush effects to make the images appear more artistic.

How do buildings and other spaces affect psychology and cognition? What are the specific features of these spaces that promote wellness? We investigate how our experience in a space can be influenced by certain design features, such as ceiling height or wall curvature.

Immersive virtual reality presents an exciting new opportunty to carry out neuroaesthetics research in a precisely configured model environment. We measure how slight adjustments to spatial features can result in striking differences for an individual's experience in that space.

In psychophysics, the relation between physical stimuli and mental phenomena is investigated. To do this properly, it is necessary to acquire objective measures of stimuli (e.g., images of portrait paintings) before showing them to observers. One approach is to create color-coded images for the orientation and strength of the edges in the image. From these images, measures for homogeneity (based on edge orientations) and complexity (based on edge strengths) for the original portrait painting are calculated.

Jackson Pollock, a key figure in the abstract expressionist movement, famously created his paintings through action, by literally dripping and pouring paint onto the canvas. This particular style of gestural abstraction was dynamic and vigorous, and emphasised the physical act of painting in itself as essential to the finished piece. We are interested in how this “movement” in abstract art is processed by viewers, and how this affects their aesthetic appreciation of the work. Stacey Humphries, a postdoc in the lab, is currently examining whether the aesthetic appreciation of action paintings changes in Parkinson’s disease patients who may have more difficulty cognitively representing action and movement. Stacey is also using fMRI to determine which brain regions are involved when healthy adults view “dynamic” vs. “static” artworks.
